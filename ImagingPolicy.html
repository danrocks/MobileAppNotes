Analysis

Policy on Recordings (Photography and Video) for Clinical and Service Use

There a couple of obvious points around the 2016 "Policy on Recordings (Photography and Video for Clinical and Service Use)". It does not seem to differ in tone and considerations from the 2008 "Photography and Video Recordings of Patients" policy and procedure or the 2011 version of this document.

Advances in both technology and policy don't seem to have been recognised. In particular:

  • The GMC Guidlines (2011) imply that recording images is a standard part of the care all doctors can provide.
  • A National Clinical Strategy for Scotland makes it clear that much wider use of technology is necesary to address the chanlanges that healthcare providers will face in coming years.
  • The absolute ubiquity of technology and its ease of use.

It is clear that the Medical Photography Service has a full understanding of the necessity of the issues around patient privact and confidentiality

It is clear the Medical Photography Service have taken on more of the responsibilty for photography. Having cross-referenced with policy documents from 2008 and 2011 this correlates with an increased side-lining of Primary care issues. Clearly a skilled Medical Photography is to be preferred where possible but these are not available in General Practice.

It is notable that this document seems entirely bureaucratic in nature. The management and contributors involved don't seem to include few if any persons from clinical or medical background. There doesn't seem to be any patient engagement either.

We think is widely understood by GPs that they are never to take photographs. By the same token, the GP is unaware of the section "8.1.1 Exceptions". This would appear to cover child/domestic abuse and, one hopes, instances of negligence or other abuse within homes.
The requirement suggests the Exception applies only to the need for Consent.
The implication is that NHS Lothian will not support a GP recording on evidence of abuse on any tool they have to hand.
The short treatment of this subject, and the fact it is not widely known, and the unethical and immoral implications of it's wording maybe explainable by nature of those who produced the document. This does not mitigate the fact that there is an unacceptable flaw in the policy.

There is a note that procedure "aspects" have been seperated from "policy aspects" - where have they gone.

Our Email

Hi John.

I've reviewed the policies you sent me. I would say that my colleagues and I take patient confidentiality, including our responsibilities enshrined in the the data-protection act, very seriously. The eHealth Security Policy guides us well in this regard. We are also happy to work within policies prescribed by the bureaucracy. However, it is not acceptable for these policies to be lazily written or to ignore current realities. There maybe a crisis in our NHS but this will not be aided by us acquising to it being kept it in stasis.

Having delved deeply into the documents, I am left with two questions:

1. How do I take digital images of dermatological conditions under the current rules?
2. How do I improve the policies so I can capture and manage such images efficiently?

In the short-term, my practice is planning to get an iPad so we can use Vision-360 on ward-rounds at care-homes. The 2016 "Policy on Recordings (Photography and Video) for Clinical and Service Use" does seem to feature a path where-by equipment must be registered and can then be used to capture images. The path isn't clear. The policy's Change Record does note that 'procedure' aspects have been removed to reduce the size of the document. Are you aware of another resource that better explains the procedure?

This system is not as efficient as it should be. I've reviewed previous versions of policies on recordings ( 2008 and 2011). The trend in these documents is that the Medical Illustration teams, part of Secondary Care, are arrogating more responsibilities to themselves. This correlates with a rising disregard for the needs of doctors and patients at the Primary Care level (the recognition that Primary Care even exists is expunged in 2016). The recent increase in quality and ubiquity of everyday tehnology seems to be ignored entirely. It is worth noting that the policy seems to have been produced without input from GPs or patient advocacy groups. General Practitioners are not even on the distribution list. Clearly, making policy is easy i.e. a ban on mobile phones was introduced 10 or more years ago. Unfortunately improving it e.g. recognising that smart phones provide opportunties, is less attractive to policy makers.

Essentially, the data-protection thrust of the policies is quite reasonable. There is scant regard for clinical outcomes, the approach taken to consent is unusual for General Practice, and the method of implementation is entirely inappropriate for 2017. The idea that best practice should be to have an additional piece of kit, to spend time physically plugging it in and out of other equipment, to move and manage patient data by hand is ridiculous: it is ineffiecient, laborious and error-prone. I want to commision an efficient imaging-app for my smart phone that I can use to improve care of my patients while adhering to the eHealth Security Policy. How can you help me do this?

I think what we are aiming to do is well within the scope of A National Clinical Strategy for Scotland. I hope you are able to help, or at least point me in the right direction.
